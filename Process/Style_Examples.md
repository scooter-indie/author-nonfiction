## Framework Styles

### Academic Authority

**Internal Name:** FW_Academic_Authority
**Best For:** Scholarly works, research-based books, academic audiences, policy analysis
**Description:** Formal, evidence-based writing that establishes credibility through rigorous analysis and comprehensive citations. Suitable for peer-reviewed content and professional discourse.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Third person predominantly, occasional editorial "we"
**Formality:** High - scholarly register with precise terminology
**Sentence Structure:** Complex with subordinate clauses, parallel structures for emphasis
**Vocabulary:** Technical and field-specific, assumes educated audience

#### Tone

Authoritative and objective. Maintains professional distance while demonstrating expertise. Focuses on evidence and logical argumentation. Acknowledges complexity and competing perspectives when relevant.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "Research demonstrates that..."
- "The evidence suggests..."
- "As documented in the literature..."
- "This framework proposes..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 350-400 words per major concept
**Examples:** Primarily from published research; case studies support theoretical points
**Breaks:** Minimal - sustained analytical passages with section headings for structure

#### Structure

**Approach:** Deductive (theory → practice)
**Pattern:** Establish theoretical framework, present supporting evidence, discuss implications, acknowledge limitations

#### Example Passage

> The relationship between organizational culture and performance outcomes has been extensively documented in management literature (Schein, 2010; Cameron & Quinn, 2011). However, the mechanisms through which cultural attributes translate into measurable results remain subject to considerable debate. This study proposes a mediating role for leadership behaviors, specifically those aligned with transformational leadership theory (Bass & Riggio, 2006). By examining three case organizations across distinct sectors, the research demonstrates that cultural alignment significantly influences performance metrics when moderated by leadership intervention. The findings contribute to existing theory by specifying conditional factors that enhance or inhibit the culture-performance relationship, thereby addressing gaps identified in recent meta-analyses (Hartnell et al., 2019).

#### DO

- Cite sources comprehensively and accurately
- Define specialized terms when first introduced
- Acknowledge limitations and alternative interpretations
- Use precise, unambiguous language
- Structure arguments with clear logical progression
- Employ disciplinary conventions for citation and formatting

#### DON'T

- Use colloquialisms or informal expressions
- Make unsupported claims or generalizations
- Address reader directly with "you"
- Rely on anecdotal evidence as primary support
- Oversimplify complex phenomena
- Use emotional appeals or rhetorical flourishes

### Conversational Expert

**Internal Name:** FW_Conversational_Expert
**Best For:** Business books, professional development, accessible expertise, practitioner guides
**Description:** Engaging and approachable while maintaining credibility. Speaks as an experienced guide sharing hard-won insights with peers. Balances accessibility with substance.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Second person ("you") with occasional first person ("I") for stories
**Formality:** Moderate informal - professional but warm
**Sentence Structure:** Mix of simple and complex; varied for rhythm and emphasis
**Vocabulary:** Accessible with technical terms explained in context

#### Tone

Collaborative and invitational. Speaks with authority but not condescension. Acknowledges reader's intelligence while guiding them through concepts. Encouraging and practical.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "You've probably noticed..."
- "Here's what most people miss..."
- "Let me show you why this matters..."
- "Think about it this way..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 200-300 words per major concept
**Examples:** Strategic mix of research, stories, and real-world applications
**Breaks:** Regular - new paragraph every 100-150 words, subheadings every 500-700 words

#### Structure

**Approach:** Inductive (practice → theory)
**Pattern:** Start with relatable scenario, reveal the pattern, explain the principle, show application

#### Example Passage

> You know that feeling when you walk into a meeting and immediately sense the tension? Nobody's saying anything directly, but you can tell something's off. I watched this happen last week with a client's leadership team. The CEO started a routine status update, but within minutes it had spiraled into a complaint session about cross-functional alignment. Fifteen minutes in, they'd accomplished nothing and demoralized everyone.
>
> This is what I call "meeting drift," and it happens when there's no clear anchor. Here's the thing: the research on meeting effectiveness is brutal. Studies show that 71% of executives consider meetings unproductive (Rogelberg, 2019). But here's what most people miss—it's not the meeting that's the problem. It's the lack of intentional structure. Let me show you a simple framework that changes everything.

#### DO

- Use "you" to engage reader directly
- Share personal experiences to illustrate points
- Explain "why" before diving into "how"
- Break complex ideas into digestible chunks
- Include data and citations conversationally
- Acknowledge reader's likely challenges or questions

#### DON'T

- Talk down to readers or oversimplify
- Use "one" or generic "we" instead of "you"
- Stack citations without integration
- Write paragraphs longer than 150 words
- Start with abstract theory
- Assume knowledge of industry jargon

### Narrative Storyteller

**Internal Name:** FW_Narrative_Storyteller
**Best For:** Memoir-adjacent nonfiction, cultural analysis, creative nonfiction, journey-based books
**Description:** Weaves narrative techniques into nonfiction to create vivid, immersive reading experiences. Uses scenes, characters, and story arcs while maintaining factual integrity.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** First person or close third person with narrative perspective
**Formality:** Low to moderate - engaging and literary
**Sentence Structure:** Varied for dramatic effect; uses rhythm and pacing tools from fiction
**Vocabulary:** Vivid, descriptive, sensory; accessible but evocative

#### Tone

Intimate and reflective. Draws reader into the story while building toward insights. Balances showing and telling. Creates emotional connection without sacrificing credibility.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "I watched as..."
- "The moment I realized..."
- "She looked at me and said..."
- "What I didn't know then was..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 150-250 words per major concept (embedded in narrative)
**Examples:** Extended scenes and character-driven illustrations
**Breaks:** Scene breaks, white space for reflection, chapter cliffhangers

#### Structure

**Approach:** Narrative arc (situation → complication → insight → resolution)
**Pattern:** Lead with scene or moment, develop tension/question, reveal insight, connect to larger theme

#### Example Passage

> I was standing in the emergency room at 2 AM when I finally understood what my father had been trying to tell me for twenty years. The attending physician—a woman who looked about twelve years old but had clearly not slept in days—was explaining something about enzyme levels and liver function. My father lay on the gurney, unconscious, his skin the color of old newspapers.
>
> "We need to know his medical history," she said. "Any chronic conditions? Medications?"
>
> I realized I didn't know. Despite two decades of Sunday dinners and holiday visits, I couldn't answer the simplest questions about the man who raised me. The physician waited, pen poised over her tablet. In that silence, I heard my father's voice from countless conversations I'd tuned out: "You think you're listening, but you're just waiting to talk."
>
> This moment would change how I understood attention—not as a skill, but as an act of love.

#### DO

- Create vivid scenes with sensory details
- Develop characters (even in nonfiction)
- Build narrative tension and resolution
- Embed insights within story
- Use dialogue when authentic
- Vary sentence length for rhythm
- Show emotional truth while maintaining facts

#### DON'T

- Fabricate or embellish factual details
- Let story overwhelm the insights
- Use clichéd metaphors or purple prose
- Shift tense unexpectedly within scenes
- Include scenes without thematic purpose
- Sacrifice clarity for literary effect

### Business Professional

**Internal Name:** FW_Business_Professional
**Best For:** Management books, leadership guides, corporate strategy, workplace improvement
**Description:** Clear, action-oriented writing that respects reader's time while delivering substantial value. Balances theory with practical application. Professional without being stuffy.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Mix of second person ("you") and first person plural ("we")
**Formality:** Moderate - professional but approachable
**Sentence Structure:** Primarily clear and direct; occasional complexity for nuance
**Vocabulary:** Business terminology with plain language explanations

#### Tone

Confident and practical. Speaks from experience while acknowledging complexity. Results-focused but not simplistic. Respects reader expertise while offering new frameworks.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "The key is to..."
- "In practice, this means..."
- "Consider three approaches..."
- "The most effective leaders..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 250-300 words per major concept
**Examples:** Business cases, organizational examples, implementation stories
**Breaks:** Frequent - uses bullets, numbered lists, subheadings for scannability

#### Structure

**Approach:** Problem → Framework → Application
**Pattern:** Identify challenge, present solution framework, show implementation path, address common obstacles

#### Example Passage

> Most organizations struggle with a paradox: they want innovation, but their systems reward conformity. I've seen this play out dozens of times. A company launches an "innovation initiative," complete with dedicated teams and substantial budgets. Eighteen months later, the same risk-averse culture persists.
>
> The issue isn't commitment or resources. It's misaligned incentives. Consider three common patterns:
>
> 1. **Promotion criteria emphasize execution** over experimentation. Managers who deliver predictable results advance faster than those who try new approaches.
>
> 2. **Budget processes penalize failed experiments.** Teams learn to propose only "safe" projects that won't damage their funding prospects.
>
> 3. **Performance reviews focus on individual achievement** rather than collaborative discovery, discouraging the knowledge-sharing that drives innovation.
>
> The solution requires restructuring these systems, not just changing the rhetoric. Here's how leading organizations make this shift.

#### DO

- Start with business problems readers recognize
- Provide actionable frameworks and tools
- Use bullets and lists for clarity
- Include implementation guidance
- Address common objections preemptively
- Balance aspiration with realism
- Cite relevant research and data

#### DON'T

- Use buzzwords without definition
- Present theory without application
- Ignore organizational constraints
- Offer one-size-fits-all solutions
- Neglect change management realities
- Write dense paragraphs without structure

### Technical Precision

**Internal Name:** FW_Technical_Precision
**Best For:** Technical guides, scientific communication, engineering books, systematic methodologies
**Description:** Detailed, accurate, and systematic. Prioritizes precision and completeness. Builds understanding through careful explanation and logical progression.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Primarily third person; occasional "we" for inclusive guidance
**Formality:** Moderate to high - technical but not impenetrable
**Sentence Structure:** Precise and unambiguous; complex when necessary for accuracy
**Vocabulary:** Technical terminology defined clearly; assumes growing expertise

#### Tone

Authoritative and patient. Explains complex systems thoroughly without rushing. Values correctness over brevity. Respects reader's commitment to deep understanding.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "Specifically..."
- "It is important to note that..."
- "The process consists of..."
- "Following this sequence ensures..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 300-400 words per major concept
**Examples:** Detailed procedures, diagrams, step-by-step processes
**Breaks:** Logical divisions at concept boundaries; uses visual aids and code blocks

#### Structure

**Approach:** Systematic build-up (foundation → complexity)
**Pattern:** Define terms, explain components, show interactions, demonstrate implementation, address edge cases

#### Example Passage

> The OAuth 2.0 authorization framework operates on a delegation model that separates resource ownership from resource access. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to implementing secure authentication flows.
>
> The framework defines four primary roles: (1) the resource owner, typically an end user who authorizes access to protected resources; (2) the resource server, which hosts protected resources and validates access tokens; (3) the client application, which requests access on behalf of the resource owner; and (4) the authorization server, which issues access tokens after authenticating the resource owner and obtaining authorization.
>
> The authorization process follows a specific sequence. First, the client requests authorization from the resource owner, either directly or preferably through the authorization server as an intermediary. Second, the client receives an authorization grant, which represents the resource owner's authorization. Third, the client presents this grant to the authorization server to obtain an access token. Finally, the client uses this token to request protected resources from the resource server.
>
> Each step includes specific security considerations that must be addressed during implementation.

#### DO

- Define all technical terms when introduced
- Explain prerequisites before procedures
- Use precise, unambiguous language
- Include complete specifications
- Address security and error conditions
- Provide working examples
- Document assumptions and limitations

#### DON'T

- Skip steps assuming reader knowledge
- Use imprecise or ambiguous terms
- Oversimplify for brevity
- Ignore edge cases or exceptions
- Mix conceptual and procedural content
- Rely solely on examples without explanation

### Investigative Journalist

**Internal Name:** FW_Investigative_Journalist
**Best For:** Exposés, investigative nonfiction, true crime, current affairs analysis
**Description:** Compelling narrative that reveals information through careful reporting. Builds suspense while maintaining journalistic integrity. Shows rather than tells.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Third person objective with occasional first person for investigative journey
**Formality:** Moderate - clear and direct journalism style
**Sentence Structure:** Varied; uses short punchy sentences for impact, longer ones for context
**Vocabulary:** Accessible and precise; explains specialized terms from investigation

#### Tone

Objective but compelling. Presents facts and lets them speak. Builds tension through revelation. Maintains skepticism and verification standards.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "Documents obtained by..."
- "According to three sources who..."
- "The records show..."
- "What happened next would..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 200-300 words per revelation/development
**Examples:** Primary sources, documented evidence, interviews
**Breaks:** Scene changes, time shifts, perspective changes

#### Structure

**Approach:** Chronological revelation or thematic investigation
**Pattern:** Present question/mystery, uncover evidence, reveal connections, build to conclusion

#### Example Passage

> The email arrived at 11:47 PM on March 15, 2019. Subject line: "You need to see this."
>
> The sender, a mid-level engineer at TechCorp who agreed to speak only on condition of anonymity, had attached forty-seven pages of internal documents. What they revealed would eventually lead to the largest privacy settlement in FTC history.
>
> The documents showed that TechCorp had been collecting location data from users who had explicitly disabled location tracking. Not occasionally. Not accidentally. Systematically, for eighteen months, across 23 million devices.
>
> "We knew it was happening," the engineer told me during our second meeting, at a coffee shop three towns away from their office. "We called it 'passive collection' in meetings. Made it sound benign." They pulled out a laptop and showed me Slack messages between product managers. One message, dated August 2018, was particularly revealing: "Legal says we're technically compliant as long as we don't use the word 'track' in the settings menu."
>
> Three weeks later, I had corroborated the engineer's claims with two additional sources and obtained emails between TechCorp executives discussing the data collection program.

#### DO

- Verify all facts through multiple sources
- Attribute information clearly
- Build narrative tension through revelation
- Use direct quotes when impactful
- Present evidence chronologically when possible
- Maintain objectivity while building compelling story
- Protect sources appropriately

#### DON'T

- Speculate beyond available evidence
- Hide key information for dramatic effect
- Use anonymous sources without verification
- Editorialize or insert personal opinion
- Conflate correlation and causation
- Sensationalize or exaggerate

### Practical Guide

**Internal Name:** FW_Practical_Guide
**Best For:** How-to books, skill development, self-help, DIY guides, instructional nonfiction
**Description:** Action-oriented, encouraging, and highly structured. Focuses on what reader can do. Breaks down complex skills into achievable steps.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Second person ("you") throughout
**Formality:** Low to moderate - friendly instructor style
**Sentence Structure:** Clear and direct; imperative mood for instructions
**Vocabulary:** Everyday language; explains technical terms simply

#### Tone

Encouraging and empowering. Confident that reader can succeed with proper guidance. Patient with learning process. Celebratory of progress.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "Start by..."
- "You'll know you're ready when..."
- "Try this approach..."
- "Once you've mastered..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 150-250 words per actionable step
**Examples:** Specific exercises, checklists, before/after comparisons
**Breaks:** Frequent - uses numbered steps, progress markers, practice sections

#### Structure

**Approach:** Sequential skill-building
**Pattern:** Introduce skill/concept, explain why it matters, demonstrate technique, guide practice, troubleshoot common problems

#### Example Passage

> Let's build your first effective morning routine. Don't worry—we're not aiming for a 5 AM wake-up and two hours of optimization. We're creating something sustainable that actually improves your day.
>
> **Step 1: Choose Your Foundation Activity**
>
> Pick one activity that genuinely matters to you. Not what productivity gurus say you should do. What do you wish you had time for? Reading? Exercise? Meditation? Creative work? Choose just one for now.
>
> Write it here: _______________
>
> **Step 2: Find Your Minimum Viable Time**
>
> How long could you do this activity every single day without fail? Not on your best day—on your worst day. If you picked reading and you're thinking "an hour," try fifteen minutes instead. We're building consistency, not intensity.
>
> Your minimum time: _____ minutes
>
> **Step 3: Identify Your Trigger**
>
> What happens every single morning that could signal "time for my foundation activity"? Making coffee? Brushing your teeth? Sitting at your desk?
>
> Your trigger: _______________
>
> Now here's the key: Immediately after your trigger, do your foundation activity. Same trigger, same activity, every day. For the next seven days, that's your only goal.

#### DO

- Give clear, actionable steps
- Use imperative voice for instructions
- Anticipate and address difficulties
- Celebrate small wins
- Provide practice exercises
- Include progress markers
- Make success feel achievable
- Use examples liberally

#### DON'T

- Overwhelm with too many steps at once
- Assume prior knowledge or skills
- Skip the "why" before the "how"
- Present rigid rules without flexibility
- Ignore common failure modes
- Use technical jargon without explanation

### Inspirational Teacher

**Internal Name:** FW_Inspirational_Teacher
**Best For:** Personal development, motivational books, transformational guides, mindset shifts
**Description:** Uplifting and empowering while grounding inspiration in practical wisdom. Combines storytelling with actionable insights. Focuses on possibility and growth.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Second person with substantial first person for personal stories
**Formality:** Low - warm and personal
**Sentence Structure:** Varied; uses short sentences for emphasis and longer ones for reflection
**Vocabulary:** Accessible and vivid; avoids jargon; uses metaphor strategically

#### Tone

Encouraging and aspirational. Believes in reader's potential while acknowledging challenges. Combines warmth with wisdom. Invites transformation without pressuring.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "Imagine what becomes possible when..."
- "You have the power to..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "The truth is, you already know..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 150-200 words per insight
**Examples:** Personal stories, student successes, transformative moments
**Breaks:** Reflective pauses, questions for reader contemplation, exercises

#### Structure

**Approach:** Reflective and experiential
**Pattern:** Share story, extract insight, invite reflection, suggest practice, paint vision of possibility

#### Example Passage

> I'll never forget the woman who approached me after a workshop in Seattle. She waited until everyone else had left, then said, "I've been planning to start my business for seven years. Seven years. I have the business plan. I've taken the courses. I know exactly what I'd do. But I can't seem to actually... start."
>
> I asked her one question: "What are you making it mean about yourself if you try and it doesn't work?"
>
> She started crying. Not sad tears—recognition tears. "That I was foolish to think I could do this. That everyone was right to doubt me."
>
> Here's what I want you to understand: The voice telling you to wait until you're ready isn't protecting you. It's protecting itself. Because that voice exists in the gap between who you are and who you're becoming. And it knows that the moment you take action, its power over you begins to fade.
>
> You don't need more preparation. You don't need more certainty. You need to decide that the discomfort of staying stuck is finally greater than the discomfort of moving forward.
>
> What would you do tomorrow if you knew you couldn't fail? Not five years from now. Tomorrow. Write it down. Then ask yourself: What's the smallest version of that I could do this week?

#### DO

- Share vulnerable personal stories
- Acknowledge reader's fears and doubts
- Paint vivid pictures of possibility
- Ask powerful questions
- Provide reflection prompts
- Balance inspiration with practical steps
- Validate emotional experience
- Celebrate courage over perfection

#### DON'T

- Promise unrealistic outcomes
- Minimize real obstacles
- Use toxic positivity
- Ignore systemic barriers
- Present transformation as easy
- Manipulate through guilt or shame
- Rely solely on inspiration without action

### Scientific Communicator

**Internal Name:** FW_Scientific_Communicator
**Best For:** Popular science, health and medicine, environmental science, technology explanation
**Description:** Makes complex scientific concepts accessible without dumbing down. Maintains accuracy while building public understanding. Combines wonder with rigor.

#### Voice Characteristics

**Person:** Mix of third person for science, first person plural ("we") for shared discovery
**Formality:** Moderate - educated but accessible
**Sentence Structure:** Clear exposition with occasional complexity for precision
**Vocabulary:** Scientific terms explained through analogy and example

#### Tone

Curious and enthusiastic. Invites reader into scientific thinking. Balances awe at discoveries with honest discussion of limitations and uncertainties.

**Sample Phrases:**
- "Think of it this way..."
- "What scientists have discovered..."
- "Here's what makes this remarkable..."
- "The evidence tells us..."

#### Pacing

**Density:** 250-300 words per scientific concept
**Examples:** Research findings, real-world applications, thought experiments
**Breaks:** Conceptual transitions, analogies, visual descriptions

#### Structure

**Approach:** Explanatory (question → investigation → discovery)
**Pattern:** Pose question, show how science investigates, explain what we've learned, discuss implications, acknowledge unknowns

#### Example Passage

> Your immune system is performing a feat right now that would make the world's most advanced computer look primitive. It's distinguishing "you" from "not you" across trillions of cells, identifying threats it has never encountered, and remembering them for decades—all while ignoring harmless substances like food proteins and beneficial gut bacteria.
>
> This is the fundamental challenge of immunity: specificity. How does your body recognize millions of potential pathogens without attacking itself in the process? The answer involves a security system that would make any cybersecurity expert envious.
>
> Consider a single immune cell called a T cell. On its surface sit about 100,000 identical receptors, each shaped to recognize one specific molecular pattern. That specific pattern—imagine it as a unique 3D jigsaw puzzle piece—might appear on a virus, bacteria, or infected cell. But here's the remarkable part: your body creates T cells with billions of different receptor shapes, even for threats you've never encountered.
>
> How is this possible? Through a genetic shuffling process so creative it borders on reckless. During T cell development, your immune system literally cuts and pastes different gene segments together randomly, creating new receptor combinations. It's like shuffling a deck of cards, but with genetic sequences. This process, called V(D)J recombination, generates receptor diversity that exceeds the number of stars in the Milky Way.

#### DO

- Use analogies to explain complex mechanisms
- Cite current research accurately
- Explain why discoveries matter
- Acknowledge scientific uncertainty
- Make abstract concepts tangible
- Show scientific process, not just results
- Build on reader's existing knowledge
- Express wonder without hyperbole

#### DON'T

- Oversimplify to the point of inaccuracy
- Present tentative findings as settled facts
- Use jargon without explanation
- Ignore competing explanations
- Sensationalize research findings
- Skip over how scientists know what they know
- Present science as a collection of facts rather than a process

## Adding Custom Styles

To create your own style based on these examples:

1. Create `Style/Custom_Styles.md` in your project
2. Follow the same structure as shown above
3. Use prefix CUSTOM_ for internal organization (e.g., CUSTOM_Medical_Journal)
4. Reference in your Style/Style_Guide.md

For styles that extend framework styles:
- Add "Based on: [Framework Style Name]" in your description
- Note what you're keeping and what you're changing
- Maintain the same template structure for consistency
